Respondents by Country
Top 10 countries represented in the survey

The survey skews heavily towards English-speaking nations, with the United States alone contributing nearly half of all responses. This reflects the geographic reach of the OSMI community.

Treatment-Seeking Overview
Proportion who sought professional mental health treatment

Roughly half of respondents sought treatment — a striking figure that underlines how prevalent mental health challenges are across the tech industry.

Age Distribution by Treatment Status
Stacked histogram — blue = sought treatment, pink = did not seek treatment

Treatment-seeking behaviour peaks in the 25–40 age bracket where career stress and life changes often converge. Younger respondents (18–25) show lower absolute treatment rates, possibly reflecting lower awareness or stigma.

Family History vs Treatment
Does family history of mental illness correlate with seeking treatment?

Respondents with a family history of mental illness are substantially more likely to have sought treatment, suggesting awareness and normalisation within families plays a key role.

Treatment Rate by Gender
% within each gender who sought mental health treatment

Women and non-binary respondents show higher treatment-seeking rates than men, aligning with broader public health findings on gendered attitudes toward mental health care.

Employer Mental Health Benefits vs Treatment
100% stacked bars showing how benefit provision relates to treatment-seeking

When employers provide mental health benefits, treatment-seeking rates are noticeably higher. Employees who are uncertain about their benefits ("Don't know") show the lowest rates, indicating that communication is as important as provision.

Company Size × Work Interference Heatmap
Frequency of respondents by firm size (rows) and how often mental health interferes with work (columns). Darker = more respondents.

Large companies (1000+ employees) and small startups (1–5) both show elevated "Sometimes" interference — pointing to very different stressors: scale & bureaucracy vs. resource scarcity & job insecurity.

Remote Work & Treatment
Do remote workers seek treatment at different rates?

Remote workers in this sample show a slightly lower treatment rate — potentially due to reduced social cues or greater flexibility in managing personal wellbeing.

Employer Support Index
% of respondents whose employer provides each support dimension

Benefits provision leads all support metrics, yet wellness programmes lag far behind — an opportunity for organisations to meaningfully improve.

Age Group × Work Interference Bubble Chart
Bubble size = number of respondents in that cell · Colour = treatment rate (light green → lavender = low → high %)

Respondents aged 32–46 with frequent work interference cluster into the highest treatment-seeking groups. Younger cohorts (18–32) with the same interference level are less likely to have sought help, raising questions about access and stigma among early-career workers.
